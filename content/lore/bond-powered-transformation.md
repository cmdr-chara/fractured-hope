# Bond-Powered Transformation

Bond-powered transformation is [[characters/paloma|Paloma]]'s primary combat ability. The manuscript explicitly describes its mechanics: her power scales with the strength of her emotional bonds, and every activation trades years of her life for the force it produces.

## The Mechanics

The source material states this directly:

> _"Her power doesn't come from anger. Comes from bonds… every time she uses it, she's trading years for raw power."_

The transformation is not a cost-free escalation. It is a deliberate sacrifice, and the sacrifice is measured in life-force — not endurance, not willpower, but actual years. The more Paloma loves, the more force she can access, and the more she pays each time she reaches for it.

This makes the ability simultaneously the most powerful thing about her and the most dangerous thing she can do to herself.

## The Bond Source

The power scales with emotional bonds, not with Paloma's individual strength or anger. The bonds that fuel it are real relationships — with [[characters/chara|Chara]], with [[characters/javier|Javier]], with the chosen family. The manuscript's framing treats this as a feature, not a flaw: the power is genuine because the bonds are genuine.

The implication is also a structural one. Losing people she loves does not weaken the transformation mechanically — she carries those bonds even after loss — but the cost of using it compounds over time.

## Arc 5 Usage

Paloma uses bond-powered transformation in Arc 5. The context is the birthday crisis — Javier's death, Frisk's death, the arrival of [[characters/hchara|HChara]]. The transformation is deployed under extreme stakes. Given the year-trading mechanic, Arc 5's usage is among the highest-cost activations in the manuscript.

> **Unresolved:** The exact number of activations and the total life-cost across the story is not detailed in reviewed sources.

## Thematic Function

Bond-powered transformation is the story's most direct statement on [[themes/chosen-family|chosen family]] as a power source. It literalizes what the story argues more abstractly: that love is not weakness, that it produces force, and that force extracted from bonds costs something real. The transformation does not let Paloma fight because she loves people. It makes her pay for that love in years.
